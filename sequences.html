Sequences & Series

Introduction

What is a sequence?

A sequence is an ordered collection of numbers.

Examples of sequences include:

  • \( 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, \dots \)
  • \( 1, 0.1, 0.01, 0.001, 0.0001, 0.00001, \dots \)
  • \( \frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{4}, \frac{1}{8}, \frac{1}{16}, \dots \)

In a sequence:

  • Numbers can be repeated.
  • Numbers do not have to follow a pattern (however, the interesting ones do!)

Sequences that follow a pattern have certain properties that can be studied further, and from there on we'll only look at those sequences.

How can we represent sequences?

For sequences that follow a pattern, we can represent them using a formula.

We can also represent a sequence using a recurrence relation.

Mathematical Definition of Convergence

Warning: This section assumes that you are familiar with the concept of a limit. The content covered in this section is typically taught in undergraduate mathematics study at college/university. A mathematical definition of convergence can be given as follows: A sequence \( x_n \) is said to be convergent to a limit \( L \) if given any real positive number \( \epsilon \) there exists an integer \( n \) such that for all \( M > n \), \( |x_M - L| < \epsilon \).
